On Georgism:

Henry George begins from an observation so simple it is often overlooked: no person creates land. We build homes, roads, and businesses, but the ground beneath them precedes all effort. Yet in modern societies, individuals are allowed to claim exclusive rights over pieces of this shared inheritance, and more importantly, to profit from their rising value.

What gives land its value is not the soil itself, but its location. A barren plot becomes valuable when people gather nearby, when roads are built, when markets emerge, when a city grows. None of this is the work of the landowner alone. It is the result of collective activity. In this sense, land value is not privately produced; it is socially generated.

From this, George draws a clear moral line. People should keep what they create, but no one should privately capture value created by everyone. When a landowner benefits from rising prices without contributing to that rise, the system quietly transfers wealth from the many to the few.

The solution he proposes is as direct as the problem: tax the value of land itself. Not the buildings upon it, not the labour invested, but the underlying location value. Such a tax does not punish production, because land cannot be hidden, moved, or reduced in supply. Instead, it reclaims for society what society has created.

In doing so, it also discourages speculation. Holding land idle becomes costly, encouraging its use or release to others. The result is a system that aligns fairness with efficiency, grounding property not in mere control, but in contribution.
